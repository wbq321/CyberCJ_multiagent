3.0: What is Internet Security?

Internet Security is the protection of digital information and systems from unauthorized access, data breaches, and cyber threats specifically related to online activities. Internet Security is important because it emphasizes the critical role of internet security in safeguarding sensitive information, preventing cybercrimes, and maintaining public safety in the digital realm.

Navigating the Web: Exploring Internet Security and Beyond.
  1. Surface Web:
    The world wide web or surface web is the part of the internet that is easily accessible and searchable through standard search engines like Google, Bing, or Yahoo. It's the web that most people interact with on a daily basis.

    The surface web itself is not vulnerable, but the vulnerability of individual websites or online platforms depends on certain factors, including their security measures, updates, and how well they protect against potential threats.

    On the defensive side, cybersecurity pros use tools to check the WWW for signs of trouble, like leaked passwords or discussions about cyber attacks. This helps them track down and be on top of problems before they become serious.
    Attackers also use the WWW to share hacking tools, plan attacks, and exchange information. This means security teams must always be alert and ready to adapt to new methods coming from attackers.


  2. The Deep Web:
    The deep web refers to the portion of the internet that isn't indexed by traditional search engines like Google, Bing, or Yahoo. It's comprised of websites that are not accessible through standard web browsers and often require specific software, configurations, or permissions to access. This part of the internet includes paywalls, databases, password-protected sites, and other content that isn't meant for public consumption.

    Now, the deep web itself isn't inherently criminal. It includes things like academic databases, private company intranets, and other legitimate platforms that aren't designed to be indexed by search engines. However, it also harbors elements of the internet that are used for illegal activities, which is where the term "dark web" often comes into play.

    In terms of criminal justice, the deep web is pertinent for a few reasons:
    1. Hidden Information:
      Criminals may use the deep web to hide information, such as their identities, communication, and illicit transactions. This hidden nature poses challenges for law enforcement agencies trying to track criminal activities and gather evidence.
    2. Investigative Leads:
      Despite being hidden, the deep web can provide valuable leads for criminal investigations. Law enforcement agencies can use specialized tools and techniques to access certain parts of the deep web, uncovering information crucial to solving cases.
    3. Cybercrime Infrastructure:
      Elements of cybercrime, such as botnets, malware distribution networks, and hacking forums, may operate within the deep web. Understanding and infiltrating these networks are essential for combating cyber threats and safeguarding digital infrastructure.


  3. The Dark Web:
    The dark web is a hidden portion of the internet that requires specific software, configurations, or permissions to access. Unlike the deep web, which includes all unindexed content, the dark web specifically refers to websites and content that exist on overlay networks and can only be accessed with specialized tools like Tor (The Onion Router) browser.

    Renowned for its notorious mask of anonymity, the Dark Web emerges as a focal point for underground transactions and illicit activities making it a hub of criminal commerce.

    The inception of the Dark Web can be traced back to the strategic endeavors of the United States (US) Department of Defense, which sought to establish a secret and secure communication channel for its intelligence operatives.

3.1.2 Threats From Web Servers

  • Malvertising

    The random ads seen on popular websites are outsourced by the owner and not always monitored for malware that is in disguise.

  • Drive-By Downloads

    Viewing a website that is already infected with malware will infect your computer. The attacker is exploiting the websites vulnerability and injecting it with harmful malicious content.

  • Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)

    An attack using scripting that originates from one site to impact another on the users computer.

  • Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF)

    An attacker takes advantage of an authentication token that a websites send to the user. This can then result in the user doing the work on the attackers behalf.

3.1.3 Transmission Risks

    Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)

    In a MITM attack, a bad actor secretly gets in the middle of a conversation between two parties, like a user and a website. They sneakily listen in on the conversation and can even change what's being said or done. It's like someone eavesdropping on your phone call without you knowing, but in the digital world. These attacks can happen on networks or even on your Wi-Fi connection, so it's important to be cautious, especially when sharing sensitive information online.

    3.2.1 Securing the Web Browser
    3.2.1.1 Security-Related Indicators

    Security-related indicators in terms of internet security refer to various signs or signals that can indicate potential security threats, vulnerabilities, or suspicious activities. These indicators are often monitored by security professionals, automated systems, or users themselves to detect and respond to security incidents effectively.


    Indicators:

    • Unauthorized Access Attempts
    • Changes in System Configurations
    • Suspicious File or Network Activity
    • Certificate Revocation
    • Security Alerts from Intrusion Detection Systems/ Intrusion Prevention Systems
    3.2.1.2 Web Browser Security Settings

    Web browser security settings refer to the configurable options and features within a web browser that help users protect themselves against various online threats and enhance their overall internet security. These settings are designed to mitigate risks such as malware infections, phishing attacks, data breaches, and unauthorized access to sensitive information.




    3.2.1.3 Managing Browser Extensions

    Managing browser extensions refers to controlling and overseeing the various add-ons, plugins, or extensions installed in your web browser. These extensions can provide additional functionality, such as ad blocking, password management, or productivity tools. However, they can also pose security risks if not managed properly.




    3.2.1.4 Virtual Private Networks (VPNs)

    A VPN functions as a fortified conduit, establishing a secure connection between your device and the internet. In this secure tunnel, your web traffic is rendered invisible to the prying eyes of hackers, shielding it from any interception attempts.


    When you access the internet, your web traffic embarks on a journey that begins with its transmission to the Internet Service Provider (ISP). En route, the traffic takes a detour through the VPN client software, where encryption wraps your data in a protective cloak. This encrypted data then travels a secure tunnel, a virtual passageway safeguarded against unauthorized access, until it reaches the VPN server. At this point, a transformation occurs where your device's IP address and true location undergo a covert change, obscuring your digital footprint. The encrypted message continues its journey through the secure tunnel until it reaches its final destination. It is at this point that the encrypted layers are decrypted, revealing the original web traffic. This thorough process ensures that your online activities remain concealed and secure, sealed from the probing eyes of potential cyber adversaries.

    3.2.2 Email Defenses

    1. Attachments:

      Attachments are considered risky because they can be used as effective vectors for delivering malware, conducting phishing attacks, exploiting software vulnerabilities, leaking sensitive data, and manipulating users through social engineering tactics. To mitigate these risks, organizations must implement robust email security measures, educate users about the dangers of email attachments, and employ best practices for handling and verifying attachments before opening them.

    2. Spam Filters:

      Spam filters are like gatekeepers for your email inbox. They're software tools that help keep out unwanted or harmful emails, like spam. They work by looking at things like who sent the email, what's in the email, and whether it looks suspicious.


    3. Email Security Settings:

      Email security settings are configurations and features that help protect your email account from various threats, such as unauthorized access, phishing attacks, malware, and spam.
