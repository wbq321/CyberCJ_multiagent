Module 1: Introduction to Cybersecurity

The Importance of Cybersecurity for Criminal Justice Professionals

In today's interconnected world, nearly every aspect of life is tied to digital technology. While this transformation brings convenience, it also exposes us to significant risks. Cybersecurity is an ongoing effort to balance technological advancements with emerging threats. Despite antivirus software and other protective measures, vulnerabilities continue to arise. For criminal justice professionals, it's crucial to understand these vulnerabilities and manage them properly. This overview will help prepare you for the follow-on modules of computer security, internet security, and privacy.

  • Global Scale of Cyber Threats:
    The consequences of breaches are severe, affecting millions of people and organizations worldwide. Constant vigilance and innovative responses are crucial to stay ahead of evolving threats.

  • Unique Challenges for Criminal Justice Professionals:
    • Universal Connectivity: Attackers can strike from anywhere, making containment challenging.
    • Rapid Technological Evolution: Millions of computers can be harnessed quickly to launch coordinated attacks.

Why are Computer Security, Internet Security, and Privacy Important?

Computer Security

Computer security is necessary to protect computer systems and their data from unauthorized access, damage, or theft.
Key benefits include:

  • Protecting Sensitive Data:
    Criminal justice systems handle highly sensitive data such as personal information, investigation details, and national security data. Effective protection is vital to prevent identity theft, compromised investigations, or data misuse.
  • Maintaining Public Trust:
    Trust in the criminal justice system hinges on protecting sensitive data. Strong security measures ensure public confidence.
  • Enhancing Investigative Capabilities:
    Understanding cybersecurity helps during the investigation of cybercrimes like hacking, identity theft, and cyberstalking, leading to more effective prevention and prosecution.
  • Compliance with Legal Standards:
    Agencies must comply with data protection and privacy regulations. Failure to do so leads to legal issues and reputational damage.

Internet Security

Internet security is critical for criminal justice professionals due to the high risk of cybercriminals exploiting online vulnerabilities.
Key considerations include:

  • Network Shared Directories:
    Shared directories are commonly used within criminal justice systems. Unauthorized access can compromise investigations and sensitive data.
  • Cloud Storage:
    Cloud storage makes data easily accessible but also vulnerable. Any data stored there could be compromised, leading to significant breaches.

Privacy

Criminal justice professionals must ensure that privacy and data management practices safeguard sensitive information.

  • Backup Strategies:
    Effective backups provide an essential safety net for restoring sensitive data after breaches. If compromised data can't be restored, investigations could be at risk.
  • Disconnect and Isolate Devices:
    Isolating devices is critical for ensuring that sensitive data remains private. Without physical disconnection, attackers could access data indirectly via connected storage.
1.1 What is Information Security?

Just as we protect our homes against burglars with a security system, we also protect our personal information against threats (such as identity theft!). Information security is the practice of protecting information with value for people and organizations. As data is transmitted, processed and stored, it must be protected.

Information security consists of three layers:

Products, People and Policy

  • Products: From door locks to sophisticated firewalls, physical products protect information.
  • People: The folks who implement and properly use security products.
  • Policy: Established by organizations so people correctly use security products.
1.2: Terminology

Terminology:

Here is a quick rundown of basic cybersecurity jargon. We will use these terms to discuss attacks and defenses.

  • Threat: action that could cause harm.
  • Asset: item with value (information in our case!).
  • Vulnerability: weakness in security.
  • Risk: exposure to danger.
  • Threat agent: person who could carry out the threat.
  • Attack vector: how the attack could occur.

Risks:

Risks have their own associated vocabulary.

  • Risk avoidance: avoid the risk in the first place, such as not storing information that could be stolen.
  • Risk acceptance: accept there's a risk and do nothing.
  • Risk deterrence: deter threat actors by advertising measures such as warnings and legal actions.
  • Risk mitigation: make the risk less serious, such as by storing information in separate locations.
  • Risk transference: hand the risk to someone else, such as by buying insurance.
1.3: Attacks and Defenses

Attacks and attackers come in many forms:

Attackers:

  • Cybercriminals:seek fortune, not fame.
  • Script kiddies: in it for thrills and notoriety.
  • Brokers: sell vulnerabilities to highest bidder.
  • Insiders: retaliate against own organization.
  • Cyberterrorists: seek to cause pani.
  • Hacktivists: think they are righting wrongs.
  • State attackers: spy on and disrupt governments and citizens.

Attacks:

  • Point-of-sale (POS) attacks, where “memory scrapers” steal card information at time of transaction.
  • Keyless entry system attacks, where threat actors amplify the signal of remote keys inside homes and gain access to vehicles parked outside.
  • Home wireless network attacks, launching malicious software on home networks through a vulnerability found in 90 major manufacturers' products.
  • Healthcare breaches are a huge problem and have even shut down hospitals entirely.

Defenses:

  • Block attacks:A strong security perimeter will protect you. Firewalls, VPNs, strong passwords, and even simple locks on doors can keep your information safer.
  • Update defenses: Always keep hardware and software updated and install operating system security updates as soon as possible to protect against known threats.
  • Minimize losses: Keep backup copies of data and have a recovery policy in place in case a successful attack occurs.
  • Stay alert: Make sure all users know how to safely use the system. Stay motivated and keep your eyes peeled! Information security is the responsibility of all users.

CIA: The Three Protections

Confidentiality

Confidentiality

Only authorized parties should be able to view the information (example: credit card information).

Integrity

Integrity

Information should be free from tampering (example: amount of purchase).

Availability

Availability

The right people need to be able to access data as needed (example: security not so complex that merchant can’t access order information).

AAA: Three Considerations

Authentication

Authentication

Ensures user is not an impostor (example: 2FA to log on)

Authorization

Authorization

Permission to use technology resources (example: clerk allowed to see order history for customer)

Accounting

Accounting

Tracks events (example: who accessed server when)
