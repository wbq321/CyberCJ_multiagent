Module 1: Introduction to Cybersecurity

1.0: Cybersecurity is Ongoing

Everything we do these days is online. You would think we could download antivirus software by now and have protection covered, right? Unfortunately, the reality is not that simple. As quickly as protections are developed, new vulnerabilities are exposed. No single product guarantees online security.

Instead, cybersecurity is an ongoing balancing act. Technologies emerge and new vulnerabilities are discovered. Those vulnerabilities are addressed, and as attacker tools improve, more risks arise. Cybersecurity professionals are constantly identifying and reacting to new threats.

As you can see below, A SINGLE ATTACK hit 1.3 billion targets. That's more than 1 in 8 people impacted globally by one threat actor! Click the image to see the full interactive version from Information is Beautiful.

The scale of cyber threats is massive. The stakes are high, and the consequences of a breach can be catastrophic. That's why cybersecurity is a critical field that requires constant vigilance and innovation to stay ahead of the threats.

Challenges of Cybersecurity

These are a few of the reasons why cybersecurity is tough to implement:

  • Devices are universally connected:
    Attackers can come from anywhere across the globe.

  • Technology keeps improving:
    Attackers can now launch attacks against millions of computers within minutes. Vast networks of attacker-controlled computers can easily overwhelm targets.

  • Attack tools are also improving:
    Attack software is available to novices and pros alike. Attack tools can vary their behavior, so the same attack looks different each time.

  • Vendors can't keep up:
    Vulnerabilities are detected quickly with these sophisticated tools. Vendors are overwhelmed trying to keep up with updating products, and security updates are challenging to implement in a timely fashion.
  • User confusion:
    Users are forced to make difficult security decisions with little instruction.
1.1 What is Information Security?

Just as we protect our homes against burglars with a security system, we also protect our personal information against threats (such as identity theft!). Information security is the practice of protecting information with value for people and organizations. As data is transmitted, processed and stored, it must be protected.

CIA: The Three Protections

Confidentiality

Confidentiality

Only authorized parties should be able to view the information (example: credit card information).

Integrity

Integrity

Information should be free from tampering (example: amount of purchase).

Availability

Availability

The right people need to be able to access data as needed (example: security not so complex that merchant can’t access order information).

AAA: Three Considerations

Authentication

Authentication

Ensures user is not an impostor (example: 2FA to log on)

Authorization

Authorization

Permission to use technology resources (example: clerk allowed to see order history for customer)

Accounting

Accounting

Tracks events (example: who accessed server when)

Information security consists of three layers:

Products, People and Policy

  • Products: From door locks to sophisticated firewalls, physical products protect information.
  • People: The folks who implement and properly use security products.
  • Policy: Established by organizations so people correctly use security products.
1.2: Terminology

Terminology:

Here's a quick rundown of basic cybersecurity jargon. We will use these terms to discuss attacks and defenses.

  • Threat: action that could cause harm.
  • Asset: item with value (information in our case!).
  • Vulnerability: weakness in security.
  • Risk: exposure to danger.
  • Threat agent: person who could carry out the threat.
  • Attack vector: how the attack could occur.

Risks:

Risks have their own associated vocabulary.

  • Risk avoidance: avoid the risk in the first place, such as not storing information that could be stolen.
  • Risk acceptance: accept there's a risk and do nothing.
  • Risk deterrence: deter threat actors by advertising measures such as warnings and legal actions.
  • Risk mitigation: make the risk less serious, such as by storing information in separate locations.
  • Risk transference: hand the risk to someone else, such as by buying insurance.
1.3: Attacks and Defenses

Attacks and attackers come in many forms:

Attackers:

  • Cybercriminals:seek fortune, not fame.
  • Script kiddies: in it for thrills and notoriety.
  • Brokers: sell vulnerabilities to highest bidder.
  • Insiders: retaliate against own organization.
  • Cyberterrorists: seek to cause pani.
  • Hacktivists: think they are righting wrongs.
  • State attackers: spy on and disrupt governments and citizens.

Attacks:

  • Point-of-sale (POS) attacks, where “memory scrapers” steal card information at time of transaction.
  • Keyless entry system attacks, where threat actors amplify the signal of remote keys inside homes and gain access to vehicles parked outside.
  • Home wireless network attacks, launching malicious software on home networks through a vulnerability found in 90 major manufacturers' products.
  • Healthcare breaches are a huge problem and have even shut down hospitals entirely.

Defenses:

  • Block attacks:A strong security perimeter will protect you. Firewalls, VPNs, strong passwords, and even simple locks on doors can keep your information safer.
  • Update defenses: Always keep hardware and software updated and install operating system security updates as soon as possible to protect against known threats.
  • Minimize losses: Keep backup copies of data and have a recovery policy in place in case a successful attack occurs.
  • Stay alert: Make sure all users know how to safely use the system. Stay motivated and keep your eyes peeled! Information security is the responsibility of all users.
1.4: Personal Security
