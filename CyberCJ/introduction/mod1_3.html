1.3 Passwords: Limitations and Best Practices

Despite being a primary means of authentication, passwords often fail to provide strong defense against attackers. The vulnerabilities stem from these common issues:

  1. Weak Passwords:
    Many users select passwords that are too simple or easily guessable, like "123456," "password," or personal information such as names and birthdays.
  2. Reused Passwords:
    People tend to reuse passwords across multiple accounts for convenience. If one account is compromised, other accounts become vulnerable.
  3. Short Passwords:
    Short passwords can be quickly cracked using brute force or dictionary attacks.
  4. Lack of Complexity:
    Passwords lacking a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols are easier to break.




To create stronger passwords, consider these best practices:
  1. Length:
    Use a minimum of 12-16 characters. Longer passwords are exponentially more secure.
  2. Complexity:
    Include uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters. Avoid predictable patterns like "1234" or "password1."
  3. Unique Passwords:
    Avoid reusing passwords across multiple accounts. Each account should have a unique password to contain any security breach.
  4. Passphrases:
    Create a passphrase using a combination of unrelated words or phrases and substitute vowels for letters. For instance, "DuskCrown!Blue42" is easy to remember, but "D4skCr0wn!Bl4342" will be much harder to guess while still being easy to remember.
  5. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):
    Use MFA when possible, combining passwords with another authentication method like SMS codes, authentication apps, or biometrics.
  6. Password Managers:
    Use a password manager to generate and store strong, unique passwords securely. This reduces the need to remember multiple passwords.
